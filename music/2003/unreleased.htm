...The Best Songs of 2003 That Weren't on the Radio

So this is that list I do every year that I don't quite know what to call. They're those songs I loved and listened to perhaps even more than the ones that make the top 100 singles list; they're the B-sides and album tracks that I loved; they're the songs that someone told me to check out and I love; they're the songs that unknown opening acts did at shows and got stuck in my head; they're songs that are nine years old but I discovered thanks to campus a capella bands. They're not even necessarily all songs made this year, but the point is that I discovered them this year. Whatever you want to call the list, these are the best of those songs.

The number one song on this list should be no surprise to anyone who knows me well. It's a nine-and-a-half minute ballad that can't be found on any of Something Corporate's CDs (okay, imports excluded -- and now I need to buy another CD...), but it's undoubtedly the band's best work. The band is also undoubtedly my favorite of the year, as you might guess by its dominance of this list with 11 entries. Likewise, the runner-up is another rarity. I discovered it when I saw Yellowcard live in September. You can finally get it on a real CD (the rereleased version of their first CD, One for the Kids.) Yellowcard ties Fountains of Wayne for the second biggest number of entries here with six each.

Aside from the obscure stuff that I doubt will ever make it on the radio, there are the album tracks that make it on this list too. Some of them may surface on radio and hit it big next year. In fact, I've heard rumors that "Mexican Wine" and "Ocean Avenue" are supposed to be released as singles soon, but they get to be on this list because they were album tracks first. Simple Plan's "Perfect" would've been in the top 10 had it not gone to radio; instead it earns a respectable placement on the top 100 singles. Two of the top 10 songs ("Unwell" and "Why Georgia") there were in the top 10 here last year, so who knows what will happen in 2004.

As usual, I give this disclaimer that this isn't highly scientific and yes, you could probably convince me to shift stuff around in the 30s, but that's not the point. The top five are definitely ranked perfectly.
