Some Basics

We'll be looking at Excel in the next few Fridays/labs. Excel is an example of spreadsheet software. Nearly everything we'll do translates pretty similarly to other spreadsheet programs, e.g. Numbers, Google Sheets.

Addresses

Row names: 1, 2, 3, ...
Column names: A, B, C, ...

Cells are at the intersection of rows and columns. The address is formed from the row and column names, e.g. A1, A2, ..., B1, B2, ...

 

Formatting Cells

Any formats applied here apply to selected cells. Available from Format > Cells or Right-click > Format Cells.

Sometimes you'll want to set cells to be specifically numeric (vs. text) or dates or other things. By default, numeric values (including dates and times) are right-aligned and non-numeric values are left-aligned.

 

Adjusting Column Widths and Row Heights

Most common way: Click and drag between row/column headers.

Inserting & Deleting Cells, Rows, and Columns

Inserting Rows or Columns

  1. Select the row (or column)
  2. Right-click the row (or column) header
  3. Select Insert

New row (or column) is above (to the left of) selected row (column).

 

Deleting Rows or Columns

  1. Select the row (or column)
  2. Right-click the row (or column) header
  3. Select Delete

 

Individual Cells

  1. Select cell
  2. Right-click the cell
  3. Select Insert or Delete
  4. Decide how to shift cells in the dialog

 

Caution

Be careful when formulas are involved. If you insert/delete in the middle of a range, the range is expanded/contracted. If you insert/delete at the boundary of a range, you may need to reprogram formulas.

Formulas: The Basics

The real power in Excel lies within the ability to use formulas. The program allows you to perform complex calculations and has many built-in functions to add to its usefulness.

Formula Bar

  • As with data and labels, you can enter formulas into cells and see them echoed in the formula bar.
  • Becomes very useful when you are entering a formula into a cell and you need to scroll in the worksheet to find the address of another cell.

 

Specifying a Formula: the framework

  1. Select the cell where you want to enter a formula
  2. Type =. That lets Excel know you are entering a formula, not text.
  3. Finish your formula
  4. Type Enter to see the results displayed.

 

Examples

  • =A5 echoes whatever is in A5 in the selected cell.
  • =A5+A6 adds the contents of A5 and A6 and puts the result in the selected cell.

 

Discussion

  • What happens if you forget the =?
  • If you change what's in A5, do you need to change the formula in the second example?

Excel Operators

There are 5 built-in (arithmetic) operators in Excel: +, -, *, /, ^. They operate on combinations of cell addresses and numeric constants.

Operator Example Meaning
+
=A1+A2
 Addition: adds the contents of A1 and the contents of A2, returns the sum
-
=A1-A2
 Subtraction: subtracts the contents of A2 from the contents of A1, returns the difference
*
=A1*A2
 Multiplication: multiplies the contents of A1 and the contents of A2, returns the product
/
=A1/A2
 Division: divides the contents of A1 by the contents of A2, returns the quotient
^
=A1^A2
 Exponentiation/Power: raises the contents of A1 to the power of the contents of A2, returns the result

Arithmetic formulas may contain:

  • one or more terms joined by any of the above operators
  • terms grouped in parentheses, such that the standard order of operations applies
  • spaces

Copying Formulas & Relative Referencing

When you copy a formula and paste it into another cell, the addresses in the formula change. The change is relative to the original cell. If the formula is pasted into a location c columns to the right of its original location and r rows below its original location, all cell references are adjusted by c columns and r rows.

Example

Suppose you initially have the formula =C3*5 + 1 in the cell D3. When it is copied and pasted into cell D4, it changes. What is the new formula?

  • D4 is one row below its original location, so the row reference is adjusted by one. 3 is the row number in the cell reference C3, so it moves down by one too, becoming C4.
  • 5 and 1 are constants; they are unchanged.

Therefore, the new formula is =C4*5 + 1.

Fill Down

You can copy and paste a formula several times, but there's a quicker way to fill a column with an isomorphic formula called Fill Down.

  1. Highlight the cell containing the original formula and as many cells below it as you like.
  2. Edit > Fill > Down or Ctrl+D.

This puts a new copy of the formula in every highlighted cell, with the references adjusted appropriately.
